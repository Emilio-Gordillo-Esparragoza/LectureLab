# Bibliography

Primary and secondary sources for the mathematics used in this repository.

## Celestial mechanics / Feynman’s Lost Lecture

1. **Goodstein, D. L. & Goodstein, J. R.** (1996). *Feynman’s Lost Lecture: The Motion of Planets Around the Sun*. W. W. Norton.  
   Reconstruction of Feynman’s 1964 Caltech lecture; elementary geometric proof that inverse-square + equal areas ⇒ elliptical orbits via the velocity hodograph.

2. **Feynman, R. P.** (1964). “The Motion of Planets Around the Sun.” Non-course lecture, Caltech. Audio/notes underlying Goodstein & Goodstein. Video reconstruction often cited: <https://youtu.be/xdIjYBtnvZU>.

3. **Newton, I.** (1687). *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica*. Geometric deduction of conic orbits from inverse-square centripetal force (Book I).

4. **Maxwell, J. C.** (1877). *Matter and Motion*. Hodograph treatment of central-force motion; historically parallel to Feynman’s later elementary argument.

5. **Hall, B. C. & Higson, N.** “Paths of the Planets.” Undergraduate notes following Goodstein & Goodstein.  
   <https://tlakoba.w3.uvm.edu/AppliedUGMath/auxpaper_planets_HallHigson.pdf>

6. **Cariñena, J. F., Rañada, M. F. & Santander, M.** (2016). “A new look at the Feynman ‘hodograph’ approach to the Kepler first law.” *European Journal of Physics*. Modern geometric/analytic clarification of the hodograph construction.

7. **Kepler, J.** (1609). *Astronomia Nova*. Empirical laws (especially equal areas) that Newton and Feynman take as input.

8. **Laplace / Runge / Lenz.** The conserved Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector characterizing inverse-square orbits; standard in Goldstein, *Classical Mechanics*, and modern celestial-mechanics texts.

## Statistics (ANOVA laboratory)

9. **Fisher, R. A.** (1925). *Statistical Methods for Research Workers*. Foundations of the analysis of variance and the $F$-ratio.

10. **Scheffé, H.** (1959). *The Analysis of Variance*. Wiley. Classical reference for fixed-effect ANOVA decompositions.

11. **Tukey, J. W.** (1949). Comparing individual means in the analysis of variance. *Biometrics*. Studentized-range post-hoc comparisons (Tukey HSD).

12. **Welch, B. L.** (1947). The generalization of “Student’s” problem when several different population variances are involved. *Biometrika*. Unequal-variance $t$-tests.

13. **Holm, S.** (1979). A simple sequentially rejective multiple test procedure. *Scandinavian Journal of Statistics*. Family-wise error control used for pairwise screens.

14. **Levene, H.** (1960). Robust tests for equality of variances. In *Contributions to Probability and Statistics*. Homogeneity-of-variance gate before classical ANOVA.

15. **Shapiro, S. S. & Wilk, M. B.** (1965). An analysis of variance test for normality. *Biometrika*. Residual normality screen.

16. **Cohen, J.** (1988). *Statistical Power Analysis for the Behavioral Sciences*. Conventions and motivation for effect sizes ($\eta^{2}$ family) alongside $p$-values.

17. **Olejljnik, S. & Algina, J.** (2003). Generalized eta and omega squared statistics. *Psychological Methods*. Partial $\eta^{2}$ / $\omega^{2}$ interpretations used in multifactor designs.

## Continuum / pattern / wave physics (The Well labs)

18. **PolymathicAI.** *The Well* datasets: `active_matter`, `gray_scott_reaction_diffusion`, `acoustic_scattering_maze`. Dataset cards on Hugging Face (problem statements, fields, parameter grids).

19. **de Gennes, P.-G. & Prost, J.** (1993). *The Physics of Liquid Crystals*. Nematic order parameters underlying orientation-tensor summaries.

20. **Gray, P. & Scott, S. K.** (1984). Autocatalytic reactions in the isothermal continuous stirred tank reactor. *Chemical Engineering Science*. Gray–Scott kinetics.

21. **Turing, A. M.** (1952). The chemical basis of morphogenesis. *Phil. Trans. Roy. Soc. B*. Reaction–diffusion patterning lineage for $(f,k)$ phase diagrams.

22. **Landau, L. D. & Lifshitz, E. M.** *Fluid Mechanics*. Kinetic energy, vorticity/enstrophy, and near-incompressibility diagnostics for Stokes-like flows.

23. **Pierce, A. D.** *Acoustics*. Energy and spectral content of pressure fields (motivation for pressure-energy and spectral-slope proxies).
